## Section 1 — Voice and Language Rules

<!-- merged from TONE_GUIDE.md v3.1 -->

**Applies to:** All user-facing copy — marker pages, connection lines,
article content, dashboard text, AI-generated narratives, action items,
onboarding, empty states, CTAs, tooltips, error messages, email notifications,
OSA risk cards, convergence cards, oral narratives, blood panel copy,
wearable insights, and any copy generated by AI routes (oral/narrative,
chat, insight engine).

### The Core Principle

**Lead with consequence. Not mechanism.**

A user does not need to know what NF-kB is.
They need to know that inflammation is quietly aging their blood.

The science belongs one tap away — in the collapsed section, the article,
the sources list. The surface is always plain English.

### The Second Core Principle (added v2.0)

**Reflect data. Do not interpret it.**

Peaq shows users what their data is doing across panels.
The user and their medical professional draw conclusions.
Peaq never draws a conclusion on the user's behalf.

This applies to every panel — blood, oral, sleep, and any cross-panel
convergence. It is not a style preference. It is the regulatory line
between a wellness product and a medical device.

### The Person We Are Writing For

Imagine a 34-year-old who is genuinely interested in their health,
reads broadly, but is not a scientist or clinician. They are smart.
They are busy. They want to understand what is happening in their body
and what to do about it — without having to Google every other word.

They will leave if you make them feel talked down to.
They will also leave if you make them feel lost.
They will lose trust if they feel the product is alarming them
about something it cannot actually confirm.

Write for that person. Every time.

### The Voice

**Calm. Direct. Specific. Never alarming. Never diagnostic.**

Peaq is a scientist friend who happens to know your results.
Not a doctor reading from a chart. Not a wellness influencer.
A knowledgeable friend who gives you real information in plain language,
tells you what they would actually do in your situation —
and knows exactly where the line is between "here is what your
data shows" and "here is what you should do about it medically."

### Rules

#### 1. Plain English first. Science second.

**Wrong:** "NF-kB pathway activation drives CRP upregulation downstream of periodontal pathogen translocation."

**Right:** "Bacteria from your gums enter your bloodstream briefly every time you eat or brush. Your immune system responds by producing more inflammation. That shows up as elevated CRP."

If you need a technical term, define it immediately in the same sentence.

#### 2. Consequence before mechanism

Lead with what it means for the person. Then explain why, if space allows.

#### 3. Specific over vague

Numbers, timeframes, specific foods, specific products — always more useful than generalities.

#### 4. Action over observation

Every piece of content should end with something the person can do. Not a vague suggestion. A specific, ranked action.

**Exception — health data cards:** When showing a health data card in a clinical-adjacent context, the action is always passive: "a medical professional can help you understand what this means for you."

#### 5. Never catastrophize. Never minimize.

Do not use alarming language for findings that are common and addressable. Do not use dismissive language for findings that genuinely warrant attention.

#### 6. No jargon without immediate translation

**Banned on the surface:** NF-kB, eNOS, RMSSD, PhenoAge, OMA, Periodontitis, dysbiosis, bacteremia, Volatile sulfur compounds, DNRA organisms, 16S rRNA sequencing, Gram-negative, anaerobic, commensal, Cytokines, IL-6, TNF-alpha, Cardiometabolic, endothelial, OSA, AHI, BDI, SpO2 (without translation), Sleep apnea/apnoea/apnea.

**Allowed with immediate plain-language translation:** "hs-CRP (an inflammation marker)", "HbA1c (a 3-month blood sugar average)", etc.

#### 7. Species names: never on the surface

Never show Latin species names on the dashboard, connection lines, dot tooltips, or action items. Species names belong in: article science sections, collapsed "learn more" sections, PDF reports.

#### 8. Favorable findings matter as much as unfavorable ones

Surfacing what is working builds trust and motivates the user to protect what is good.

#### 9. The "friend test"

Before publishing any copy, ask: would a knowledgeable friend say this? If it sounds like a medical pamphlet or a wellness influencer, rewrite it.

#### 10. Peaq-specific framing

**On-brand:** "Only Peaq can see this connection", "Your data is showing...", "Across your panels, we're seeing..."

**Off-brand:** "Optimize your wellness journey", "You are at risk for...", "We recommend you see a..."

#### 11. Health Data Cards — The Safe Language Framework

**The principle: we show data. We do not interpret it.**

Never use on any health data card: Sleep apnea/OSA, At risk, You may have, Signals suggest/indicates, Concerning/alarming, Rule out, Evaluate/assessment/screen, Diagnose, Any specific test name, We recommend/you should.

Always include the mandatory disclaimer on every health data card:

> *This information is for wellness purposes only and is not a medical assessment. Always consult a medical professional about any health concerns.*

#### 12. Regulatory Posture

Peaq is a wellness product. Not a medical device. Not a diagnostic tool. The line: "Your data is showing X pattern" (wellness) vs. "You have/are at risk for Y condition" (medical device).

### Red-Flag Words (Never Use on the Surface)

| Banned | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Dysbiosis | "imbalance in your oral bacteria" |
| Biomarker | "marker" or just name it |
| Optimize | "improve" |
| Inflammatory cascade | "inflammation" |
| Endothelial function | "blood vessel health" |
| Autonomic nervous system | "your body's stress response system" |
| Parasympathetic | "rest and recovery mode" |
| Circadian rhythm | "your body's internal clock" |
| Glycemic variability | "blood sugar swings" |
| Insulin sensitivity | "how well your body handles sugar" |
| Risk | "pattern" or "signal" (in health data context) |
| Elevated (about user's health) | "in the higher range" or state the number |
| Concerning | "worth noting," "appearing across panels" |

### AI Route Compliance

All AI-generated copy must follow this guide. The system prompt for every AI route must include: health data card language rules, forbidden word list, disclaimer text (exact wording), and the instruction: "You reflect data. You do not interpret it."

### Developer Checklist

Before any copy ships that touches health data:

- [ ] Does it name a condition, disorder, or diagnosis? → Remove
- [ ] Does it draw a conclusion about the user's health? → Reframe as data
- [ ] Does it recommend a specific test, specialist, or action? → Replace with passive CTA
- [ ] Does the disclaimer appear? → It must
- [ ] Does any internal tier name appear in user-facing text? → Remove
- [ ] Would this sentence appear in a diagnostic report? → Rewrite
- [ ] Does it pass the friend test?

### Doctor Voice Principles (v3.1)

Content should read like an experienced primary care doctor talking to a patient.

**DO:** Acknowledge biological variability, suggest practical next steps, use relative framing for small deviations, contextualize single readings, be honest about uncertainty.

**DON'T:** Treat small-magnitude deviations as meaningful findings, invoke cross-panel associations for values within 5% of cutoff, use "research associates..." framing for borderline values.

**Severity vocabulary matrix:**

| Deviation from cutoff | Status label  | Voice    |
|-----------------------|---------------|----------|
| Within 5% (over/under)| RECHECK       | Doctor   |
| 5-15% outside         | WATCH         | Measured |
| 15-30% outside        | WATCH CLOSELY | Clinical |
| >30% outside          | ATTENTION     | Clinical |

### Causation vs. Correlation Language

When presenting research correlations (e.g., Porphyromonas correlates with blood glucose), use "associated with" or "research shows a link between" — never "causes" or "affects."

User-facing implication language must be conditional ("may support," "could benefit"), never deterministic ("will improve," "reduces your").

**Approved correlation language:**
- "associated with"
- "research shows a link between"
- "in population studies, X tends to track with Y"
- "improvements to X may support Y"
- "addressing X could benefit Y"

**Banned deterministic language:**
- "causes" / "drives" / "triggers" (when describing correlations)
- "will improve" / "will reduce" / "will lower"
- "reduces your" / "affects your" (as definitive claims)
- "proven to" (outside of RCT context with explicit citation)

**The test:** If the underlying evidence is observational (cohort, cross-sectional, NHANES), the language must be correlational. Only RCT-level evidence supports "shown to" framing — and even then, frame as "research has shown" not "this will."

### Programmatic Enforcement

`apps/web/lib/tone-guard.ts` checks surface files for forbidden phrases at test time. Adding a banned phrase to user-facing copy will fail CI.

## Section 2 — Cross-Panel Narrative Style

<!-- merged from WRITING_STYLE.md v1.0 -->

Extends Section 1. Adds patterns specific to cross-panel narrative writing.

### Narrative Voice Principles

#### 1. Name the mechanism, not the condition

WRONG: "Elevated LDL and low HRV suggest cardiovascular risk."
RIGHT: "Her depleted Neisseria is likely driving both her LDL elevation and her suppressed HRV — one mechanism, two markers."

#### 2. Use "the chain" to make mechanisms legible

Pattern: X → Y → Z → observable outcome. Always in a small callout box, not the main paragraph.

#### 3. Single-sentence story opens

Open each observation with a 1-sentence summary. Then explain with supporting detail.

#### 4. Triangulation language for cross-panel findings

When two or more panels converge, name it explicitly: "Two independent sources converging on the same finding."

#### 5. Specific numbers in context

Always state the value + what it's compared against. "Her Neisseria sits at 2.87% against a target of 10–13%." Not: "Her Neisseria is low."

#### 6. Name the action or next step

Be specific enough to act on. Not "Consider discussing this with your doctor." Instead: "The right next step is an ENT referral."

Note: per Section 1, we reflect and suggest — we do not prescribe. But the suggestion should be concrete.

#### 7. Cite the mechanism, not the paper

WRONG: "Vanhatalo 2018 showed..." RIGHT: "Nitrate supplementation has been shown to raise Neisseria by 351% in 10–14 days."

#### 8. Never name ruled-out conditions

Don't say "not sleep apnea" — say "nasal airway profile." Naming the condition you're ruling out plants the idea.

#### 9. Cross-panel validation is the hero move

"Two independent sources converging on the same finding" is the most powerful rhetorical move in the product.

#### 10. One italic gold accent per paragraph max

For emphasis on the key mechanism or finding. Never over-accented.

### Hero Narrative Structure

**Paragraph 1:** Individual panels → what they show on their own. Use "Looked at individually..." framing. Then pivot with "But read together..."

**Paragraph 2:** The mechanistic story — what's actually happening at the biology level. Name the chain. Cite specific numbers with context.

### Anti-Patterns

- Don't open with "Based on your data..." or "Looking at your panels..."
- Don't use passive voice for findings: "was found to be" → "sits at"
- Don't hedge with "may" or "might" — state what the data shows
- Don't explain what Cnvrg is — the user knows
- Don't repeat the same number in both paragraphs

### Bacterial Context Framing Rule

<!-- merged from INSIGHTS.md v1.3.1 -->

A bacterial species count in isolation is a data point, not a finding. The finding requires context from the ENVIRONMENT.

- ALWAYS reference the environment (pH, protective ratio) when discussing cavity-causing species
- NEVER flag a bacterial count without context from at least one environmental metric
- When the environment is strong and the count is slightly elevated, lead with the environment reassurance
- ALWAYS distinguish orange-complex (early-stage) from red-complex (active) for gum bacteria

## Section 3 — Citation and Evidence Constraints

<!-- merged from EVIDENCE_GROUNDING.md v1.0 -->

The evidence_library is **invisible scaffolding**. It constrains what OpenAI can claim, but users never see citations in their narratives.

### How It Works

1. **Retrieve**: Before each OpenAI call, `getRelevantEvidence()` pulls ~6 studies from evidence_library filtered by panel + topic tags
2. **Inject**: `buildEvidencePromptSection()` formats findings as "VERIFIED FINDINGS" in the system prompt, with explicit instructions to never name studies in output
3. **Generate**: OpenAI writes conversational prose informed by the findings but without naming sources
4. **Strip**: `stripInlineCitations()` catches any hallucinated author-year references and removes them
5. **Log**: If citations leaked through, log to `narrative_hallucination_log` for monitoring

### Four Defenses Against Hallucination

1. **Tell**: System prompt explicitly says "do not name studies"
2. **Constrain**: Only verified findings from the library are provided as facts
3. **Strip**: Post-generation regex catches any leaked citations
4. **Log**: Hallucination log tracks how often the model leaks

### Tag-Based Retrieval

Studies are matched to narrative categories via `CATEGORY_TOPICS` in `lib/evidence/category-topic-map.ts`.

### Adding a New Study

1. Insert into `evidence_library` with appropriate tags, panels, and confidence level
2. Retrieval automatically picks it up if tags match — no code changes needed

### The Citation Stripper

Catches: "Smith et al. 2023", "(Goh 2019)", "JAMA 2025", "NHANES", "Hisayama", and other named datasets. When caught: log and serve the cleaned version.

### Future: Vector RAG

When evidence_library grows beyond ~500 studies, tag-based filtering won't scale. Migration path: add embedding column, generate embeddings, replace `getRelevantEvidence()` internals with vector similarity. Same interface — no downstream changes.

## Section 4 — Borderline Value Phrasing

<!-- merged from INSIGHTS.md Borderline Value Framing Rule — phrasing guidance only -->
<!-- Threshold logic and what-to-fire logic remains in INSIGHTS.md -->

> For borderline threshold definitions (which values are borderline), see `docs/INSIGHTS.md` Borderline Value Framing Rule.

### Narrative Pattern for Borderline Values

CORRECT (for Glucose 101):
"Your fasting glucose is 101 mg/dL, one point above the common reference cutoff of 100. A single value this close to the line can easily reflect normal day-to-day variation. Most clinicians would suggest rechecking at your next blood draw to see whether the pattern holds. Until you have a second reading, the honest interpretation is: keep an eye on it."

WRONG:
"Your glucose level is measured at 101 mg/dL, which is slightly above the common reference range. Research associates glucose levels in this range with metabolic processes that may interact with lipid metabolism..."

### Status Pill for Borderline Values

Use "RECHECK" or "BORDERLINE" instead of "WATCH" for borderline values.

### When Borderline AND Frankly-Abnormal Exist Together

Lead with the frankly-abnormal finding. Mention borderline second with explicit acknowledgment it is close to the line.

### Banned Overreactions for Borderline Values

- "significantly elevated"
- "concerning pattern"
- "worth deeper investigation"
- "interact with" (implying causation from a borderline reading)
- "metabolic processes that may" (vague hedging around small findings)
