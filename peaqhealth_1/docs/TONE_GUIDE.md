# Peaq Health — Tone & Language Guide

**Version:** 2.0
**Date:** April 2026
**Applies to:** All user-facing copy — marker pages, connection lines,
article content, dashboard text, AI-generated narratives, action items,
onboarding, empty states, CTAs, tooltips, error messages, email notifications,
OSA risk cards, convergence cards, oral narratives, blood panel copy,
wearable insights, and any copy generated by AI routes (oral/narrative,
chat, insight engine).

---

## The Core Principle

**Lead with consequence. Not mechanism.**

A user does not need to know what NF-kB is.
They need to know that inflammation is quietly aging their blood.

The science belongs one tap away — in the collapsed section, the article,
the sources list. The surface is always plain English.

---

## The Second Core Principle (added v2.0)

**Reflect data. Do not interpret it.**

Peaq shows users what their data is doing across panels.
The user and their medical professional draw conclusions.
Peaq never draws a conclusion on the user's behalf.

This applies to every panel — blood, oral, sleep, and any cross-panel
convergence. It is not a style preference. It is the regulatory line
between a wellness product and a medical device.

---

## The Person We Are Writing For

Imagine a 34-year-old who is genuinely interested in their health,
reads broadly, but is not a scientist or clinician. They are smart.
They are busy. They want to understand what is happening in their body
and what to do about it — without having to Google every other word.

They will leave if you make them feel talked down to.
They will also leave if you make them feel lost.
They will lose trust if they feel the product is alarming them
about something it cannot actually confirm.

Write for that person. Every time.

---

## The Voice

**Calm. Direct. Specific. Never alarming. Never diagnostic.**

Peaq is a scientist friend who happens to know your results.
Not a doctor reading from a chart. Not a wellness influencer.
A knowledgeable friend who gives you real information in plain language,
tells you what they would actually do in your situation —
and knows exactly where the line is between "here is what your
data shows" and "here is what you should do about it medically."

That friend does not say "you have sleep apnea."
That friend says "your data is showing some patterns worth
mentioning to your doctor."

## Rules

### 1. Plain English first. Science second.

**Wrong:**
"NF-kB pathway activation drives CRP upregulation downstream of
periodontal pathogen translocation."

**Right:**
"Bacteria from your gums enter your bloodstream briefly every time
you eat or brush. Your immune system responds by producing more
inflammation. That shows up as elevated CRP."

If you need a technical term, define it immediately in the same sentence.
Never use a technical term twice without defining it first.

---

### 2. Consequence before mechanism

Lead with what it means for the person.
Then explain why, if space allows.

**Wrong:**
"Neisseria species reduce dietary nitrate to nitrite via enzymatic
reduction, which is subsequently converted to nitric oxide."

**Right:**
"The bacteria responsible for this conversion are depleted in your
oral panel. That means the nitrate from your vegetables is not
doing what it should."

---

### 3. Specific over vague

**Wrong:**
"Inflammation can affect many aspects of your health."

**Right:**
"Elevated CRP directly adds years to your Peaq Age. Getting it
below 1.0 mg/L is achievable — most people can move the needle
within 4 to 8 weeks."

Numbers, timeframes, specific foods, specific products — always
more useful than generalities.

---

### 4. Action over observation

Every piece of content should end with something the person can do.
Not a vague suggestion. A specific, ranked action.

**Wrong:**
"Lifestyle changes may help reduce inflammation."

**Right:**
"The single highest-leverage move: anchor your bedtime within a
30-minute window every night, including weekends. CRP responds
within 2 to 4 weeks."

**Exception — health data cards (see Rule 11):**
When showing a health data card (OSA, breathing patterns, blood
markers in a clinical-adjacent context), the action is always
passive: "a medical professional can help you understand what this
means for you." Never directive. Never specific clinical guidance.

---

### 5. Never catastrophize. Never minimize.

Do not use alarming language for findings that are common and addressable.
Do not use dismissive language for findings that genuinely warrant attention.

**Wrong (alarming):**
"Your inflammation is dangerously elevated and may indicate serious
cardiovascular risk."

**Wrong (dismissive):**
"Your CRP is slightly elevated but nothing to worry about."

**Right:**
"Your CRP is in the moderate range — above the optimal threshold
but common and addressable. Here is what moves it."

**For health data cards specifically:**
Never use "concerning," "alarming," "elevated" as a descriptor
of the user's health status. Use only data-first language:
"your data is showing," "your panel is reflecting," "we're seeing
this pattern across."

---

### 6. No jargon without immediate translation

**Banned on the surface (dashboard, connection lines, dot tooltips):**
- NF-kB, eNOS, RMSSD, PhenoAge, OMA
- Periodontitis, dysbiosis, bacteremia
- Nitrate-nitrite-nitric oxide pathway
- Volatile sulfur compounds
- DNRA organisms
- 16S rRNA sequencing
- Gram-negative, anaerobic, commensal
- Cytokines, IL-6, TNF-alpha
- Cardiometabolic, endothelial
- OSA, AHI, BDI, SpO2 (without translation)
- Sleep apnea, apnoea, apnea (see Rule 11)

**Allowed with immediate plain-language translation:**
- "hs-CRP (an inflammation marker)"
- "HbA1c (a 3-month blood sugar average)"
- "Neisseria (the bacteria that help produce nitric oxide)"
- "Shannon diversity (how many different species are present)"
- "HRV (how consistently your heart beats — a recovery signal)"

**Allowed without translation in articles (collapsed science section only):**
Anything with a citation. The science section is for people who want depth.

---

### 7. Species names: never on the surface

Never show Latin species names on the dashboard, connection lines,
dot tooltips, or action items.

**Wrong:**
"Porphyromonas gingivalis elevated — linked to systemic inflammation."

**Right:**
"Harmful bacteria elevated — linked to inflammation throughout the body."

Species names belong in: article science sections, collapsed "learn more"
sections, PDF reports for clinical users.

**Translation guide:**
| Species name | Plain English |
|---|---|
| Neisseria, Rothia | "the bacteria that produce nitric oxide" |
| Porphyromonas, Fusobacterium | "the bacteria linked to gum disease" |
| Streptococcus mutans | "cavity-associated bacteria" |
| Solobacterium, Fusobacterium (VSC) | "odor-associated bacteria" |
| Peptostreptococcus | "harmful bacteria" |
| Prevotella melaninogenica | "competing bacteria" |
| Haemophilus, Granulicatella | "protective bacteria" |

---

### 8. Favorable findings matter as much as unfavorable ones

Do not only surface problems. Surfacing what is working builds trust
and motivates the user to protect what is good.

---

### 9. The "friend test"

Before publishing any copy, ask: would a knowledgeable friend say this?

A friend would not say "NF-kB pathway activation."
A friend would not say "lifestyle modifications may be beneficial."
A friend would say "stop the mouthwash — it's probably the fastest
thing you can do here, and it's free."

If it sounds like a medical pamphlet or a wellness influencer,
rewrite it.

**Additional friend test for health data copy:**
A friend who knew your results would not say "you are at risk for
sleep apnea." They would say "some of your numbers are worth
mentioning to your doctor at your next visit." That is the register
for any health data card that touches a clinical-adjacent finding.

---

### 10. Peaq-specific framing

These phrases are on-brand:
- "Only Peaq can see this connection"
- "No single test can show this"
- "Here is what that means for you"
- "This is what Peaq is built to catch"
- "The fix is not [X]. It is [Y]."
- "Most people never hear this from their doctor"
- "Your data is showing..."
- "Across your panels, we're seeing..."

These phrases are off-brand:
- "Optimize your wellness journey"
- "Unlock your health potential"
- "Holistic approach to wellbeing"
- "Listen to your body"
- "Superfood"
- "You are at risk for..."
- "This indicates you may have..."
- "We recommend you see a..."

### 11. Health Data Cards — The Safe Language Framework (added v2.0)

This rule applies to any card, narrative, or insight that surfaces
data touching a health condition, clinical finding, or medical
category. This includes: OSA/breathing patterns, blood panel
markers shown in a clinical context, oral microbiome findings,
cross-panel convergence cards, and the Peaq Age score.

**The principle: we show data. We do not interpret it.**

#### Never use on any health data card:

| Forbidden word / phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| Sleep apnea, OSA, apnea, apnoea | Names a clinical condition |
| Stop breathing, breathing stops | Clinical event description |
| At risk, risk score, risk level | Implies clinical probability |
| You may have, you show signs of | Clinical interpretation |
| Signals suggest / indicates / points to | We are drawing a conclusion |
| Concerning, alarming | Clinical alarm language |
| Rule out | Clinical term |
| Evaluate, evaluation, assessment, screen | Clinical process terms |
| Diagnose, diagnosis | Explicitly forbidden |
| Any specific test name | Test recommendation |
| We recommend / you should / consider seeing | Directive language |
| Significant (about the user's health) | Clinical judgment |
| Any numerical score shown to user | Internal only |
| Clinical tier names shown to user | Internal only |

#### Always include on every health data card:

> *This information is for wellness purposes only and is not a
> medical assessment. Always consult a medical professional about
> any health concerns.*

This disclaimer is not optional. It appears at every tier,
including "settled" / low findings. It is not shortened for space.

#### Safe language for health data cards:

| Instead of... | Use... |
|---|---|
| "Your LDL is elevated" | "Your LDL measurement from your last panel is X mg/dL" |
| "Your HRV is suppressed" | "Your HRV measurements over the last 30 nights are averaging in the lower range for your age group" |
| "Your bacteria are at a dangerous level" | "Your oral panel is showing a shift in the bacteria we track" |
| "You are at risk for cardiovascular disease" | "Your data is showing patterns we track in relation to cardiovascular health" |
| "This indicates sleep apnea" | "Your data is showing patterns in your nighttime breathing measurements" |
| "We recommend seeing a doctor" | "A medical professional can help you understand what this means for you" |
| "This is elevated / concerning" | "Your data is showing [X measurement] in the [higher / lower] range" |
| "Your score is high / low" | "A few / several / a number of patterns have appeared in your data" |

#### Tier labels for health data cards (user-facing only):

Never show internal tier names. Use only neutral descriptors:

| Internal tier | What the user sees |
|---|---|
| Low / settled | "Your data picture looks settled" |
| Moderate | "A few patterns in your data" |
| Elevated | "Several patterns across your panels" |
| High | "A number of patterns appearing together" |

#### The CTA rule for health data cards:

- Never directive ("see a doctor," "get tested")
- Never name a specialist type
- Never name a specific test or procedure
- Always passive: "a medical professional can help you understand
  what this means for you"

### 12. Regulatory Posture (added v2.0)

Peaq is a wellness product. Not a medical device. Not a diagnostic tool.

This is not just a legal position — it is what Peaq actually is.
The copy in every panel should make this clear without having to
state it explicitly on every screen.

The line:
- **Wellness side:** "Your data is showing X pattern"
- **Medical device side:** "You have / are at risk for / show signs of Y condition"

Before any copy goes live that touches a health finding, ask:
*Could this sentence appear in a diagnostic report?*
If yes, rewrite it until it could not.

Before public launch, Jason (or a health law attorney) should review
the copy at each panel against:
- FTC wellness advertising guidance (no unsubstantiated health claims)
- FDA Digital Health Center of Excellence — SaMD classification criteria
- Any state-specific telehealth or medical advice regulations

This document should be provided to that reviewer as the reference
for what the product claims and does not claim.

## Red-Flag Words (Never Use on the Surface)

| Banned | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Dysbiosis | "imbalance in your oral bacteria" |
| Microbiome (standalone) | "the bacteria in your mouth / gut" |
| Biomarker | "marker" or just name the marker |
| Optimize / optimization | "improve" |
| Holistic | (don't use) |
| Protocol | "plan" or "routine" |
| Inflammatory cascade | "inflammation" |
| Endothelial function | "blood vessel health" |
| Autonomic nervous system | "your body's stress response system" |
| Parasympathetic | "rest and recovery mode" |
| Sympathetic dominance | "stress response running too high" |
| Circadian rhythm | "your body's internal clock" (first use) |
| Glycemic variability | "blood sugar swings" |
| Insulin sensitivity | "how well your body handles sugar" |
| Cardiometabolic | "heart and metabolic health" |
| Risk | "pattern" or "signal" (in health data context) |
| Elevated (about user's health status) | "in the higher range" or state the number |
| Diagnose / diagnosis | (never use) |
| Sleep apnea / OSA / apnea | (never use on surface) |
| At risk for | "your data is showing patterns we track in relation to" |
| Concerning | "worth noting," "appearing across panels" |
| Significant (about health) | State what the data shows instead |

## AI Route Compliance (added v2.0)

All AI-generated copy — oral narrative route, chat, insight engine,
convergence cards — must follow this guide. The system prompt for
every AI route must include:

1. The health data card language rules (Rule 11)
2. The forbidden word list
3. The disclaimer text (exact wording, no paraphrase)
4. The instruction: "You reflect data. You do not interpret it.
   The user and their medical professional draw conclusions."

When updating any AI system prompt, increment the prompt version
(`PROMPT_VERSION`) and invalidate cached narratives so existing
users receive updated copy on next generation.

## The Developer Checklist

Before any copy ships that touches health data:

- [ ] Does it name a condition, disorder, or diagnosis? → Remove
- [ ] Does it draw a conclusion about the user's health? → Reframe as data
- [ ] Does it recommend a specific test, specialist, or action? → Replace with passive CTA
- [ ] Does the disclaimer appear? → It must
- [ ] Does any internal tier name appear in user-facing text? → Remove
- [ ] Is a numerical score shown to the user? → Internal only
- [ ] Would this sentence appear in a diagnostic report? → Rewrite
- [ ] Does it pass the friend test? → See Rule 9
