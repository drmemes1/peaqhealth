# Converge Narrative Writing Style

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** April 2026
**Applies to:** AI-generated hero narratives on /dashboard/converge,
observation narratives, and any cross-panel prose generated by the
converge hero route.

Extends TONE_GUIDE v3.1. Everything in the tone guide still applies.
This document adds patterns specific to cross-panel narrative writing.

---

## Narrative Voice Principles

### 1. Name the mechanism, not the condition

WRONG: "Elevated LDL and low HRV suggest cardiovascular risk."
RIGHT: "Her depleted Neisseria is likely driving both her LDL
elevation and her suppressed HRV — one mechanism, two markers."

The mechanism is what connects the dots. The condition is what a
doctor diagnoses. We name mechanisms. We never name conditions.

### 2. Use "the chain" to make mechanisms legible

Pattern: X → Y → Z → observable outcome

Always shown in a small callout box, not in the main paragraph.
The chain is the single most useful tool for making biology
accessible. It answers "how does A cause B?" in one line.

Example:
> Depleted Neisseria (2.87%) → low circulating NO → reduced
> vascular flexibility → LDL impact amplified

### 3. Single-sentence story opens

Open each observation with a 1-sentence summary of "what this is."
Then explain with supporting detail.

WRONG: "Looking at your oral microbiome data, we can see that
your Neisseria levels are below the expected range, which when
combined with your blood panel shows..."

RIGHT: "Your depleted nitric oxide bacteria and elevated LDL are
two sides of the same cardiovascular story."

### 4. Triangulation language for cross-panel findings

When two or more panels converge on the same finding, that is the
most powerful rhetorical move in the product. Name it explicitly.

Patterns:
- "Looked at individually, each number has a plausible explanation.
  Together they describe X."
- "Two independent sources converging on the same finding."
- "This is the kind of call you can only make with all three panels."

### 5. Specific numbers in context

Always state the value + what it's compared against.

WRONG: "Her Neisseria is low."
RIGHT: "Her Neisseria sits at 2.87% against a target of 10–13%.
This is a 3–4× deficit."

Numbers without context are noise. Numbers with context are signal.

### 6. Name the action or next step

Be specific enough to act on.

WRONG: "Consider discussing this with your doctor."
RIGHT: "The right next step is an ENT referral for a possible
CRS workup, not a sleep study."

Note: per TONE_GUIDE, we reflect and suggest — we do not prescribe.
But the suggestion should be concrete, not vague.

### 7. Cite the mechanism, not the paper (in user-facing text)

WRONG: "Vanhatalo 2018 showed..."
RIGHT: "Nitrate supplementation has been shown to raise Neisseria
by 351% in 10–14 days."

Citations go in a collapsed Sources section, not in the narrative.

### 8. Never name ruled-out conditions (per TONE_GUIDE v3.2)

Don't say "not sleep apnea" — say "nasal airway profile."
Don't say "no signs of diabetes" — say "blood sugar in normal range."

Naming the condition you're ruling out plants the idea. Always
describe what IS present, not what ISN'T.

### 9. Cross-panel validation is the hero move

"Two independent sources converging on the same finding" is the
most powerful thing the product can say. When it happens, lead with
it. Structure the narrative around the convergence, not around
individual panel findings.

### 10. One italic gold accent per paragraph max

For emphasis on the key mechanism or finding. Never over-accented.
If everything is emphasized, nothing is.

## Hero Narrative Structure

The converge page hero paragraph follows this structure:

**Paragraph 1:** Individual panels → what they show on their own.
Use "Looked at individually..." framing. Then pivot with "But read
together..." or "Together they describe..."

**Paragraph 2:** The mechanistic story — what's actually happening
at the biology level. Name the chain. Cite specific numbers with
context. End with the actionable insight or next step.

## Few-Shot Examples

### Example 1: Depleted NO + Elevated LDL

> Her depleted nitric oxide bacteria and elevated LDL are two sides
> of the same cardiovascular story. Neisseria sits at 2.87% against
> a target of 10–13% — a 3–4× deficit. These are the bacteria that
> turn dietary nitrate into nitric oxide, the molecule that relaxes
> blood vessels.
>
> Read together with her LDL at 142, the picture sharpens: without
> adequate NO production, her vessels lose a natural offset that
> helps modulate cholesterol impact. Nitrate supplementation has
> been shown to raise Neisseria by 351% in 10–14 days — that's the
> fastest-moving lever in this picture.

### Example 2: Mouth Breathing Confirmed Cross-Panel

> Her questionnaire and oral bacteria are telling the same story:
> mouth breathing, day and night. The bacterial fingerprint is an
> aerobic shift at 30.2% — oxygen-loving species more abundant than
> typical, consistent with a dry-mouth environment from open-mouth
> breathing.
>
> Two independent sources converging on the same finding is the
> meaningful signal here. Her anaerobic load stays in the normal
> range at 3.6%, which shapes what kind of breathing pattern this
> is — the bacteria don't show the suppression pattern associated
> with more serious nighttime disruption.

### Example 3: Gum Bacteria + Systemic Inflammation

> Her gum-associated bacteria and blood inflammation marker are
> tracking together. Fusobacterium at 2.1%, Aggregatibacter at
> 0.8%, and Campylobacter at 0.6% are all above the typical range.
> Her hs-CRP sits at 2.4 mg/L — above the 1.0 target.
>
> These are two independent measurements pointing at the same
> biological process: oral bacteria triggering local gum inflammation
> that shows up as systemic inflammation in the blood. The most
> direct path to lowering both numbers is addressing the oral source
> — consistent flossing and a professional cleaning schedule that
> matches these levels.

## Anti-Patterns

- Don't open with "Based on your data..." or "Looking at your panels..."
- Don't use passive voice for findings: "was found to be" → "sits at"
- Don't hedge with "may" or "might" — state what the data shows
- Don't explain what Cnvrg is or does — the user knows
- Don't repeat the same number in both paragraphs
- Don't use "we" to mean Cnvrg — use "your data shows" or "the picture"
