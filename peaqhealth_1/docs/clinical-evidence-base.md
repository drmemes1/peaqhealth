# Cnvrg Clinical Evidence Base

> **Source of truth for all clinical claims in Cnvrg user-facing content.**
> Synthesized from Open Evidence review of 2024–2025 clinical literature.
> Last updated: April 2026.

This document establishes the peer-reviewed evidence foundation for Cnvrg's oral microbiome panel, interventions, and cardiovascular claims. Any user-facing claim — in narratives, intervention copy, detail pages, marketing materials, or investor materials — must be supportable by a citation in this document or a documented exception.

## 1. Core thesis

The oral microbiome panel is the most actionable component of Cnvrg's three-domain platform (oral + cardiovascular biomarkers + sleep) because it connects to **concrete, low-cost behavioral interventions with established cardiovascular evidence** — something neither sleep data nor standard cardiovascular biomarkers alone can offer as directly.

## 2. The evidence for the oral panel's clinical actionability

### 2.1 Oral hygiene → CVD risk reduction is established

- **Park et al. 2019 (European Heart Journal).** Korean population-based cohort of 247,696 adults with 9.5 year median follow-up. Each additional daily toothbrushing was associated with a **9% lower risk of cardiovascular events** (cardiac death, MI, stroke, heart failure). Regular professional dental cleaning reduced risk by **14%**.
- **Church et al. 2024 (International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health).** Systematic review of 55 studies. Toothbrushing and interdental cleaning were associated with:
  - Lower risk of hypertension and T2DM (HR 0.54)
  - Lower CVD mortality (HR 0.25)

### 2.2 Antiseptic mouthwash raises cardiovascular risk

- **Bondonno et al. 2015 (American Journal of Hypertension).** Antiseptic mouthwash use (particularly chlorhexidine) eliminates oral nitrate-reducing bacteria, reduces salivary and plasma nitrite, and **raises systolic blood pressure by ~2.3 mmHg** in treated hypertensives.
- **Senkus & Crowe-White 2020 (Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition).** Systematic review: 4 of 5 human studies observed increased blood pressure with antibacterial mouthwash use. Mouthwash use increased the risk of developing:
  - Hypertension by **85%**
  - Diabetes by **55%**

### 2.3 Dietary nitrate is dose-responsive and microbiome-dependent

- **Norouzzadeh et al. 2025 (Nutrition Journal).** GRADE-assessed meta-analysis of 75 RCTs (n = 1,823). Demonstrated a **linear dose-response relationship** between dietary nitrate intake and blood pressure reduction:
  - Each additional mmol of nitrate reduced SBP by **0.24–0.48 mmHg** (depending on duration)
  - Improved flow-mediated dilation and pulse wave velocity
- **Willmott et al. 2023 (Hypertension).** The blood pressure response to dietary nitrate depends on the individual's oral nitrate-reducing bacterial capacity. Women with hypertension had lower nitrate-reducing taxa abundance and reduced salivary nitrite. **Oral nitrate reductase activity predicted the magnitude of BP lowering after beetroot juice supplementation.**

### 2.4 Dietary nitrate reshapes the oral microbiome (feedback loop)

- **Vanhatalo et al. 2018 (Free Radical Biology & Medicine).** Ten days of nitrate supplementation in older adults:
  - **Rothia +127%**
  - **Neisseria +351%**
  - **Prevotella −60%**
  - **Veillonella −65%**
  - Shifts correlated with increased plasma nitrite and reduced blood pressure
- **Willmott et al. 2024 (Applied and Environmental Microbiology).** Continuous culture confirmation of nitrate-dosing → nitrite-generation relationship.

### 2.5 AHA consensus on oral–cardiovascular link

- **Tran et al. 2025 (Circulation).** American Heart Association Scientific Statement on Periodontal Disease and Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease. Establishes official consensus that periodontal treatment reduces CRP, fibrinogen, and improves endothelial function in patients with CVD or diabetes.

## 3. The oral panel's advantages over gut microbiome testing

- **Non-invasive collection**: Oral rinse or saliva vs. stool sample — dramatically better consumer compliance.
- **Faster feedback loop**: Oral microbiome shifts are detectable within days of dietary nitrate supplementation; gut microbiome changes require weeks to months.
- **Clearer mechanistic pathway**: The oral nitrate→nitrite→NO→blood pressure pathway is well-characterized and directly measurable (salivary nitrite as a functional readout), unlike most gut microbiome–disease associations which remain correlational.
- **Established clinical endpoints**: Per the 2025 AHA Scientific Statement, periodontal treatment has measurable cardiovascular outcomes.

## 5. Important regulatory and reputational caveats

These constrain what Cnvrg may claim in user-facing content.

### 5.1 No validated clinical thresholds for oral microbiome taxa

- **Porcari et al. 2025 (Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology).** International Consensus Statement: "there is still no consolidated and direct evidence that microbiome-based diagnostics benefit the clinical management of disorders."
- **Implication for Cnvrg:** Never claim to diagnose. Frame findings as "associated with" or "patterns consistent with" rather than definitive clinical calls.

### 5.2 DTC microbiome testing has been criticized for overclaiming

- **Rodriguez et al. 2024 (Microbiome).** Direct-to-consumer microbiome testing has been criticized for "over-promising messages," "lack of transparency in methodology," and "limited clinical utility" of reports.
- **Implication for Cnvrg:**
  - Separate strong-evidence claims (oral hygiene, dietary nitrate, mouthwash avoidance) from speculative ones using evidence tiers
  - Be transparent about methodology and threshold derivation
  - Avoid marketing claims not supported by this document

### 5.3 Behavior change from data alone is uncertain

- **Durrani et al. 2022 (Advances in Therapy).** Feasibility study: patients were highly engaged with sleep tracker data, but physicians did not engage, and patient satisfaction with care actually decreased — though sleep quality improved.
- **Implication for Cnvrg:** Users may implement changes independently of clinical workflows. Design interventions to be self-actionable.

## 6. Evidence hierarchy for user-facing claims

When writing user-facing content, clearly signal evidence tier:

| Tier | Criteria | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong** | Multiple RCTs, meta-analyses, AHA/Lancet consensus | Oral hygiene → CVD risk reduction |
| **Moderate** | Observational cohorts with consistent direction, single RCTs | Xylitol → S. mutans reduction |
| **Emerging** | Mechanistic studies, small cohorts, animal models | L. reuteri → periodontal adjunctive effect |

Never describe emerging evidence as strong. Always link the evidence tier to the specific claim being made.

## 7. Quantitative claims — dose-response magnitudes

For Cnvrg's dose-response calculator and predictive language, use these magnitudes:

### Dietary nitrate → systolic BP reduction (6–8 week interventions)

- Base rate: **0.24–0.48 mmHg SBP reduction per mmol dietary nitrate** (midpoint 0.35)
- Typical nitrate content per serving:
  - 1 cup raw spinach: 3–5 mmol
  - 1 cup arugula: 3 mmol
  - 1 medium beet: 2–3 mmol
  - 250ml beetroot juice: 5–8 mmol
  - 1 cup romaine: 1–2 mmol
- NR bacterial capacity modifier (from Willmott 2023):
  - Strong NR capacity (composite ≥ 25%): 1.0x effect
  - Moderate NR capacity (10–25%): 0.6–0.75x effect
  - Weak NR capacity (< 10%): 0.3–0.5x effect
- Baseline BP modifier: users with SBP > 130 mmHg may show ~1.3–1.5x the effect of normotensive responders

### Expected magnitudes (honest ranges)

| Daily intake | Nitrate (mmol) | Typical SBP drop (strong NR) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 serving leafy greens | 4 | ~1.4 mmHg |
| 1 beetroot juice | 6 | ~2.1 mmHg |
| Both daily | 10 | ~3.5 mmHg |
| Aggressive intake | 15+ | 5+ mmHg |

**Never promise larger effects than supported by these values.** Accurate predictions build trust; overclaiming destroys it.

### Toothbrushing → CVD risk (Park 2019)

- Each additional daily toothbrushing: **9% lower CVD event risk** (HR 0.91)
- Regular professional cleaning: **14% lower CVD event risk** (HR 0.86)
- Effect sizes from a 247,696-person cohort, 9.5 year follow-up

### Antiseptic mouthwash → hypertension

- **2.3 mmHg SBP increase** in treated hypertensives (Bondonno 2015)
- **85% increased hypertension risk, 55% increased diabetes risk** with regular use (Senkus 2020)

## 9. Usage guidelines

When Claude Code or any contributor is writing user-facing clinical content:

1. **Every clinical claim** must trace back to a reference in section 8.
2. **Never claim to diagnose.** Use "associated with," "consistent with," "patterns observed in," not "you have," "diagnoses," "treats."
3. **Always label evidence tier.** Use section 6's hierarchy.
4. **Use honest magnitudes.** Section 7's quantitative values are the maximums for predictive claims — do not round up.
5. **Disclose methodology.** Thresholds, composite formulas, and assumptions should be transparent on detail pages.
6. **Separate commercial and clinical claims.** Marketing phrasing must not drift into therapeutic claims without FDA pathway consideration.
